Origin Story

How UTA Racing Was Born

In 1982, the Formula SAE competition introduced a new Briggs & Stratton class to increase participation. UTA entered with two Mini Baja cars and dominated — taking the top two spots. Had they been allowed in the Formula class, they would have finished first and third.

In 1983, new rules demanded a "formula-style body." The team's solution: wrap aircraft fabric around the nose and sides and call it a body. They tuned their 11 HP engines up to 13 HP by fitting motorcycle carburetors and removing governors, and made up ground in the corners what they lacked on the straight.

Then in 1984, the Formula SAE competition moved to Arlington — home turf. Dr. Robert Woods, who would go on to found UTA Racing and shape the competition itself, helped rewrite the rules into the format teams follow today.

First Formula Car
F84 — The First True Formula Car

UTA competed for the first time with a true formula car. F84 featured a continuously variable transmission and a Honda inline 4-cylinder air-cooled 400cc engine. Despite dominating the performance portion, the car finished third — setting the stage for a championship the following year.

1985
🏆 Championship
First Formula SAE Championship

Returning with a Honda V4 500cc engine, UTA took first place and claimed their first Formula class championship. This car also introduced a hand-operated sequential "butterfly" shifter that combined clutching and shifting into a single motion — a signature UTA innovation.

F85 · Honda V4 500cc

1986
🏆 Championship
Back-to-Back — Defending Champions

UTA defended their title in 1986, cementing themselves as one of the early powerhouses of the Formula SAE competition. The back-to-back championships established a culture of winning that would define the program for decades.

1990 – 1998
The Dynasty
98
1990
🏆 Championship
Return to the Top

After a competitive stretch, UTA recaptured the championship in 1990, signaling the start of a dominant run through the decade. The team's consistency and engineering depth set them apart from the growing field of competitors.

1991
🏆 Championship
Three-Year Streak Begins

1991 brought another championship, making it back-to-back titles again. UTA had become, alongside Cornell University, one of only two programs that could consistently contend for the top spot across multiple decades.

1995
🏆 Championship
Championship No. 6

UTA claimed yet another national title, building on a legacy that already included six US championships. The team's ability to recruit, design, and execute at the highest level year after year made them one of the most respected programs in FSAE history.

F95 Photo
1996
🏆 Championship
Final US Championship of the Era

UTA's eighth and final US championship came in 1996. The team nearly scored a perfect 150 design points the following year — only one point behind École de Technologie Supérieure — demonstrating their engineering prowess extended well beyond raw speed.

1998
🇬🇧 International Inaugural Win
Formula Student England — Inaugural Champions

UTA traveled to England to compete in the very first Formula Student competition, co-sanctioned by IMeche and SAE International. Competing against UK universities and two other US teams, UTA won the inaugural event — making them the first-ever Formula Student champions.

🇯🇵 Japan
Formula SAE Japan Champions

A third international championship, this time in Japan. UTA became one of the only programs in FSAE history to claim titles on three separate continents — a testament to a program that could compete anywhere, against anyone.

2004 FSAE Japan
2012
The Petsche Center for Automotive Engineering

Arlington businessman Arnold E. Petsche endowed $1 million — matched through UTA's Maverick Match program — to establish the Arnold E. Petsche Center for Automotive Engineering in Woolf Hall. The facility houses a showroom displaying several of UTA's championship cars, along with meeting and workspace for the team.

2013
F13 — 5th in World, 1st in the US

F13 placed fifth at the Formula SAE Lincoln competition among a field of international teams, and was ranked first in the United States — a strong return to prominence on the world stage.

2021
Special Project
UTA-Mod — The Autocross Weapon

With North American FSAE competition uncertain due to the pandemic, UTA Racing built an A-Modified car called UTA-Mod — designed with a single goal: the fastest raw autocross time possible. In 2024 at the SCCA Solo Nationals, the car achieved exactly that. It continues to compete in autocross events today.

F22 — Knowledge Recovery

F22 was the first UTA FSAE competitor since the Covid pandemic. The 2022 season focused on knowledge recovery and simplistic design — taking the proven 2020 engine package and refining it alongside improved frame designs and components, rebuilding team depth after years of disruption.

E25 — Electric Era Begins

UTA Racing expands its program with E25, the team's first serious electric vehicle entry in the Formula SAE Electric class. Led by Chief Engineer Lance Hill and Battery Pack Lead Grant Yanagi, E25 marks a new chapter in UTA's storied legacy.
